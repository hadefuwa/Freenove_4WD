📥 Guide: How to Download Your Code ▶

Uploading a compiled sketch

  1. In the Arduino IDE, go to Sketch → Export Compiled Binary.
  2. Open your sketch folder (Ctrl+K). You will see a file ending in .uf2.
  3. Drag and drop that .uf2 file directly into the RPI-RP2 drive.
  4. The Pico will automatically reboot and start running your code.

If you can't see the Pi on your PC then...

  1. Unplug the Pico from your computer.
  2. Press and hold the BOOTSEL button on the Pico board.
  3. While holding the button, plug the USB cable back in.
  4. Release the button. Your computer should now show a new drive named RPI-RP2.

01 — First Movesmotors, servos, sound, lights, and eyes
🏎️ 01.1_Car_Move_and_Turn Car Move & Turn ▶
📖 How it works

This program teaches the robot car to drive in every direction using its four special "mecanum" wheels, which have angled rollers that let the car go forward, spin in place, slide sideways, or glide diagonally — all without turning the wheels like a steering wheel.

The "Magic Command":

Motor_M_Move(LeftFront, LeftRear, RightFront, RightRear);
  • Positive numbers (1 to 100): Wheel goes forward.
  • Negative numbers (-1 to -100): Wheel goes backward.
  • delay(1000): Wait for 1000 milliseconds (1 second).

The real lesson is in the pattern of numbers: matching signs make the car go straight, opposite left/right signs make it spin, and opposite front/back signs make it slide sideways or diagonally.

🏆 Try it: Challenges
🏆 Challenge 1: The Speed Racer

Goal: Make the car go twice as fast when moving forward, and super slow when moving backward.

How-to: Look at the first Motor_M_Move line. Change the 50s to a higher number (up to 100).

Tip: If 100 is the fastest, what number would make the car crawl like a turtle?
🕒 Challenge 2: The Long Haul

Goal: Make the car drive forward for 3 seconds instead of just 1.

How-to: Find the delay(1000); line right after the forward command. Change the number inside the parentheses.

Tip: The computer counts in milliseconds. 1000 = 1 second, so 3000 = 3 seconds.
🔄 Challenge 3: The "Dizzy" Spinner

Goal: Make the car spin in a circle in one spot (a "Zero Turn").

How-to: To spin, one side needs to go forward while the other goes backward. Try:

Motor_M_Move(50, 50, -50, -50);
Tip: If the car just turns slightly, increase the delay so it keeps spinning longer!

👀 01.5_Matrix Matrix Display (Eyes) ▶
📖 How it works

Those two squares on the front of your car are like the robot's "eyes." Each 8×8 grid of tiny lights is drawn using bytes — one byte per row, where each of its 8 bits is either a 0 (LED off) or 1 (LED on), written compactly in hex like 0x18. The program stores several of these 8-row "frames" in arrays (x_array, y_array) and loops through them to create a simple flipbook-style animation.

It teaches arrays, binary/hex number representation, bit patterns, and using loops to repeat an action over and over forever. Here are 5 missions for you to try!

Can you make the robot wink one eye while keeping the other one wide open? Look at y_array (the right eye).

Goal: Copy the first line of numbers and paste it over the second line so they match.

Secret: If the right eye stays the same but x_array keeps changing, it will look like a wink!
🌙 Mission 3: Night Mode

Find matrix.init(0x71); inside void setup.

Goal: Right under that line, add: matrix.setBrightness(1);

Secret: Numbers range from 0 (off) to 15 (super bright). Try 15 to see the difference!
😠 Mission 4: Angry Robot

Let's make the eyes look like flat, grumpy lines. Look at x_array and y_array.

Goal: Change one row of numbers to:

02 — Ultrasonic Sensingbat-style sonar, then obstacle avoidance
📡 02.1_Ultrasonic_Ranging Ultrasonic Ranging ▶
📖 How it works

This program makes the ultrasonic sensor "look around" by riding on a servo that swings it right, center, left, and back to center, over and over. Each time it stops at a new angle, the code sends out a tiny, silent sound pulse and times how long the echo takes to bounce back — just like a bat uses echolocation — then does some quick math (distance = time × speed of sound ÷ 2) to turn that time into a distance in centimetres.

1. The Startup (setup): Wakes up the Serial Port, gets the sensor and servo ready, looks straight ahead at 90°.
2. The Scouting Loop: Look Left (150°) → Look Center (90°) → Look Right (30°) → Look Center again (90°) — forever.

🏆 Try it: Missions
🚀 Mission 1: The Fast Scanner 🏃

Goal: Make the robot look left, center, and right much faster.

How-to: Find all the delay(500); lines. Change 500 to 200.

Secret: Go too low (like 50) and the robot's neck might get a bit shaky!
🚀 Mission 2: The Owl Turn 🦉

Goal: Make the robot look as far left and right as it possibly can.

How-to: Change 150 to 180 (max left) and 30 to 0 (max right).

Secret: Servos usually move from 0 to 180. 90 is always the middle!
🚀 Mission 3: The Wall Alarm 🚨

Goal: Use your hand to make the robot "yell" through the Serial Monitor when you get too close.

How-to: Make the robot say "TOO CLOSE!" when the distance is less than 10.

Tip 1 — The "If" Secret: 
if (Get_Sonar() < 10) {
  Serial.print("TOO CLOSE!");
}
Tip 2: Place it right after the Get_Sonar line.
Tip 3: Sensors can see "ghost" numbers occasionally — that's just sensor noise!
🚀 Mission 4: The "Yes" Man ✅

Goal: Make the robot shake its head "yes" instead of "no" (up and down instead of left and right).

How-to: Check how your sensor is mounted — if it can't look up, rewrite the code to switch fast between just two angles: 70 and 110.

Tip 1: Try going from 80 to 100 instead of 30 to 150 for a subtle "nod."
Tip 2: Try delay(100); for a quick gesture instead of a slow turn.
Tip 3: Move the head gently by hand first to check it moves smoothly.
🚀 Mission 5: The Guard Dog 🐕

Goal: Make the robot stay at 90° for a while, do one quick scan, then go back to waiting.

How-to: At the very end of void loop(), add delay(5000);.

Tip 1: The loop runs top to bottom — put the delay at the very bottom.
Tip 2: End with Servo_1_Angle(90); so it "stares" straight ahead while waiting.
Tip 3: Change the nap time: 2000 = 2s nap, 10000 = 10s nap.

💡 Engineering Pro-Tips

  • The "90" Rule: 90° is always the "Home" position.
  • Watch the Wires: Turning to 0° or 180° can tangle or pull wires tight.
  • The Serial Monitor: Click the Magnifying Glass icon to see the distance numbers!

💡 03.1_Photosensitive Light Tracking (Reading) ▶
📖 How it works

This program uses two photoresistors (light-sensing components) to check how bright it is on the left and right sides of the robot. Every half-second, it reads each sensor's light level as a number using analogRead(), then sends both numbers to the Serial Monitor so you can compare which side is brighter or darker.

It's a great first look at reading real-world analog sensors, storing values in variables, and using a loop() that repeats forever with a delay() in between.

Level 1: The Speed Hacker (Warm-up)

Right now, the robot reads the light sensors and prints numbers twice a second.

Mission: Change the code so it reads light ten times a second, then change it to read once every two seconds.

Teaches: The delay() function and how milliseconds work (1000 ms = 1 second).

Solution: Find delay(500); → change to delay(100); (fast) or delay(2000); (slow).
Level 2: The Light Detective (Logic & If/Else)

Mission: Add an if/else inside loop(). If the left sensor's number is bigger than the right's, print "The light is on the Left!" Otherwise print "The light is on the Right!"

Teaches: Comparing two variables using >.

🧠 04.2_Track_Car IR Follow (Line-Following Car) ▶
📖 How it works

Think of this code as the "Brain" of your robot. It follows a simple loop: Look, Decide, Act. Every loop it looks at the three floor sensors, squishes their readings into one number (0–7), and uses a switch/case to decide what that number means:

  • 2 (010): Line in the middle → Drive straight.
  • 4 or 6 (100/110): Line sliding left → Turn left.
  • 1 or 3 (001/011): Line sliding right → Turn right.
  • 7 (111): Big black wall or finish line → Stop.

It then spins the four wheels — sending opposite-signed speeds to the left and right sides to pivot like a tank — and shows a matching face or arrow on the LED display.

🎮 05.2_IR_Receiver_Car IR Receiver Car (RC Driving) ▶
📖 How it works

This program turns the car into a real RC car you drive with an infrared remote! Every button sends its own secret code, and handleControl() uses a big switch/case "lookup table" to match each code to a specific move — forward, back, turn, or (in a special mode) slide diagonally sideways, which only mecanum wheels can do.

It also teaches "non-blocking timing," where the car checks the clock (millis()) instead of freezing with delay(), so its animated LED face keeps blinking while it's still listening for your next button press. If no button code is found, the code falls through to a safe "default" case that simply stops the motors.

📶 06.1_WiFi_APP_TcpServer WiFi TCP Server (Networking Basics) ▶
📖 How it works

This program turns the Pico W into a tiny WiFi "phone" that only answers calls — it connects to your home WiFi and waits on a specific "door number" called a port (4002) for a companion phone/PC app to connect. Once connected, the Pico acts like a chat bridge: anything the app sends gets printed to the Serial Monitor, and anything you type gets sent back to the app.

It teaches WiFi networking basics (SSID/password, IP addresses, ports), how a TCP server waits for and accepts connections, and how programs check ("poll") repeatedly for new data using functions like client.connected() and client.available().

📱 06.2_Multi_Functional_Car Multi-Functional Car (Full WiFi App Control) ▶
📖 How it works

This is the "everything" program — it lets you control the whole car from a phone app over WiFi, all in one sketch! Your phone sends short text messages like N#45#80#0#0, and the Pico W reads that text, breaks it into pieces ("parsing"), and calls the right function to drive the mecanum wheels, change the LED face's expression, light up the RGB strip, or check the battery and sensors.

It teaches how a computer can run several "brains" at once without freezing — using millis() timers instead of delay() so the face animates, the lights glow, and new WiFi commands keep arriving all at the same time — plus it shows off some real trigonometry, letting a joystick's angle and distance become sideways, forwards, and spinning motion for the mecanum wheels.
